Company Process Guide

Updated 26 Mar 2026 from the first 15 DOCX files in the AV process ingest. Focus: clarity-first marketing, specialist sales posture, and client-prep guardrails. Labels show current best-known owners while conflicts remain under review.
1
Start with a pillar theme David Nancy
Pick one buyer pain point or flagship blog per cycle. Map it to the target industry/city before anything else so every downstream post feels bespoke, not templated.
2
Build the calendar as an execution tool
Lay out 10–45 day runs across LinkedIn (Matt + Company), Facebook, YouTube clips, and optional TikTok/shorts. Calendars show sequencing, not policy—rewrite them once the campaign window closes.
3
Reuse with edits only
When new direction is light, mine the approved archive but rewrite the hook, CTA, and timestamped lines. Tone stays formal/journalistic. No legacy memes or stale holiday jokes.
4
Pair every post with a visual asset
Use demo stills, case-study clips, or industry-specific renders. Legal/medical posts still require the template overlay + contact block per the onboarding guide.
5
Local SEO + Google Business shadow
Each pillar blog should spawn Google Business posts and city-specific snippets. Keep them keyword-rich but human. Link back to AustinVisuals.com with a clear CTA.
6
Tool + approval reality check
MeetEdgar / Buffer / Hootsuite all appear in legacy docs; confirm the active scheduler before onboarding someone new. Matt + Nancy remain final approvers until the matrix is updated.
1
Subject line first
Pick the promise and write the subject/title before drafting copy. Audience = time-poor male decision-makers (marketing directors, owners, product leads) who care about speed, clarity, and status.
2
Lead with value, then sell Nancy
Use blog posts for raw material but add something new: cost tips, data, or a sharper example. No “Austin Visuals is great” opener—prove usefulness first, sell later.
3
Manual build + approvals
Copy is finalized in Docs, then pasted manually into Mailchimp. Wednesday-night draft ➜ Thursday 9 AM send is the current fast cadence, but one SOP still says “every two months.” Default to monthly until leadership confirms.
4
Proof and assets
Use charts, pricing tables, or before/after frames when possible; quantitative proof is specifically requested in the workflow doc. Matt approves until a new editor of record is named.
5
Repurpose downstream
Each newsletter should create at least one LinkedIn thought post and one local SEO snippet to reinforce the theme.
1
Pain-first ideation
Pick prompts that echo real buyer friction: clarity for regulators, faster sales decks, technical accuracy, trade-show polish. City/industry angle must be baked in, not tacked on.
2
Pre-write assets
Gather client logos, best-of visuals, bios, and proof points before drafting so the article reads bespoke. Austin Visuals call-outs (3× per article) are still a working SEO rule until disproven.
3
Draft ➜ CTA ➜ Repurpose
Write the long-form post, close with a direct CTA, then slice it into newsletter sections, social posts, and Google Business blurbs. One pillar article should fuel an entire content cycle.
4
Tooling
Topic generators (Portent, HubSpot) and infogr.am are listed, but the active stack needs reconfirmation. Keep a running list of what’s actually in use.
1
Specialist pitch Matt
Austin Visuals sells clarity for complex products. Core message: agencies resell production, AV is the direct specialist who keeps technical accuracy intact across many styles.
2
Segment lens
Mid-enterprise marketing and product leaders are prime; agencies are both competitors and partners. Adjust vocabulary for technical, executive, marketing-led, or procurement-led buyers.
3
Qualification checklist
Budget, Authority, Need, Timing plus Input Readiness. Ask about script/story, runtime, brand kit, references, CAD/engineering files, field photos, and objective/use case (trade show, training, investor, regulator).
4
Documentation
Discovery notes go into the intake form + CRM before promise-making. Hidden stakeholders or missing assets get flagged before pricing.
5
Sales→Production handoff
Only after deposit + confirmed inputs. Include objectives, audience, deliverables, and whether concept/storyboard step was purchased.
1
Diagnose hesitation
Most pushback = unclear vision, fear of cost overrun, or missing stakeholder buy-in. Surface which one it is before replying.
2
Offer low-friction starters
Concept design, style frames, or storyboard packages convert “let us think about it” into a smaller, faster paid step. Make these options explicit on every hesitant lead.
3
Modernize the legacy script
The old cold-call script is relationship-first ("checking in, here's what’s new"). Keep that tone but update the language before reactivation pushes go live.
4
Close the loop
Every objection path ends with a defined next action (scheduled design sprint, budget revisit, or polite exit). Log outcomes in the CRM immediately.
1
Minimum viable inputs
Script or outline, desired runtime, brand kit, examples of desired + off-limits styles, project objective. Missing items = higher quote or slower kickoff.
2
Technical add-ons
For engineering/industrial/medical jobs: CAD files, drawings, field photos, SME access for review. Intake checklist should branch for technical vs general marketing.
3
Revision discipline
Two structured rounds by default. Capture every note in writing, confirm receipt, and tie comments back to the objectives defined at intake.
4
Archive + reuse
Finished work feeds the “Best Work Samples” sheet plus future marketing assets. Tag each deliverable with industry, use case, and whether the client allows public use.
